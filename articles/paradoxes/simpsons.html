Simpson's Paradox

Aggregated metrics can lead to confident, but completely wrong, decisions.

Apr 25, 2026 – Arman Boyacı

A Minimal Example

A: ●●○○○ → 2/5 = 40%

B: ●●●○○ → 3/5 = 60%

Conclusion: B looks better than A.

Group 1

A₁: ● → 1/1 = 100%

B₁: ●●●○ → 3/4 = 75%

Group 2

A₂: ●○○○ → 1/4 = 25%

B₂: ○ → 0/1 = 0%

Now A is better in both groups.

The Root Cause

In the above example, imbalanced weights across subgroups of the data skewed the aggregate results.

In general, the root cause of Simpson’s paradox is a confounder variable. A cofounder affects both depenedent and indepent variables. Ignoring confounders may lead to misleading conclusions. From this perspective, Simpson's paradox is not a paradox but rather a statistical modeling issue.
