Monty Hall

Use the extra information properly for the optimal decision.

Feb 14, 2026 – Arman Boyacı

Monty Hall problem is the following: there are three doors, one contains a car and other two contain goats. As a contestent, we choose one door. The host, who knows which door has the car, reveals one of the remaining doors that contain a goat and offers a choice: We can either stick with our initial choice or switch to the other unopened door.

What should we do?

Most people who encounter this problem decide to stick with their initial choice. The main reason is they think there are two remaining doors, and since the probabilities were initially were equal, nothing has changed after the host reveals one door.

They assume there is a 50-50 chance of winning. Psycologically, it also feels worse to switch and lose than to stick and lose.

This reasoning is incorrect. We will show in three different ways that switcing is the better strategy:

  1. Story Proof A story proof is a proof by interpretation. For counting problems, this often means counting the same thing in two different ways, rather than doing tedious algebra. A story proof often avoids messy calculations and goes further than an algebraic proof toward explaining why the result is true. The word “story” has several meanings, some more mathematical than others, but a story proof (in the sense in which we’re using the term) is a fully valid mathematical proof. - From Joe Blitzstein, Introduction To Probability, page 20.
  2. Proof using the law of total probabilities
  3. A computer simulation

Story Proof

If we were allowed to pick two doors at the start, our probability of winning would be 2/3. While the rules do not permit choosing two doors outright, host's elimination of one unselected door effectively fivues that opportunity. Switcing is equivalant to having bet on the two doors we did not originally choose.

Mathematical Proof

Without loss of generality, assume we initially picked Door 1. Let $I_1$ denote this information.

Define:

  • $S$: success by switching
  • $D_j$: Door $j$ contains the car

Using the law of total probability:

$$ P(S \mid I_1) = P(S \mid D_1, I_1)P(D_1) + P(S \mid D_2, I_1)P(D_2) + P(S \mid D_3, I_1)P(D_3) $$

Evaluate each term:

  • If the car is behind Door 1, switching loses: $P(S \mid D_1, I_1) = 0$
  • If the car is behind Door 2, switching wins: $P(S \mid D_2, I_1) = 1$
  • If the car is behind Door 3, switching wins: $P(S \mid D_3, I_1) = 1$

Since each door initially has probability $\frac{1}{3}$:

$$ P(S \mid I_1) = 0 \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{2}{3} $$

Therefore, switching yields a winning probability of $\frac{2}{3}$, which is strictly greater than staying ($\frac{1}{3}$).

Computer Simulation

              

import numpy as np
import random
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def extensive_test(tries,switch):    # bool switch-says weather to switch or not
  ctr=0
  ans=[]
  for i in range(tries):
    arr=[0,0,0]
    car_pos=random.randint(0,2)   
    arr[car_pos]=1              # making a random arrangement of two goats (0) and one car (1)
    nums=[0,1,2]
    initial_selection=random.randint(0,2)  # making a random initial selection
    nums.remove(initial_selection)
    for j in nums:
      if(arr[j]==0):
        nums.remove(j)                    # opening one of the 2 remaining doors that hides a goat
        break
    if(switch):                       # in case of switching change selection to other door
      selection=nums[0]
    else:
      selection=initial_selection       # otherwise sticking to the same door
    if(arr[selection]==1):
      ctr+=1
    ans.append(ctr)
  return ans
  
size=10000
switch=extensive_test(size,True)
noswitch=extensive_test(size,False)

plt.plot(list(range(size)),switch)
plt.plot(list(range(size)),noswitch)
plt.axis([0,size,0,size])
plt.xlabel("number of tries")
plt.ylabel("number of wins")
